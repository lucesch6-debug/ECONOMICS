Remark 1 We converge towards an equilibrium, where everyone answers O. This is the Nash equilibrium of the game (explained in plenary lecture). Why O is the Nash equilibrium9 If everyone chooses O, the target is O, and O is the closest possible guess no player can improve by changing their answer. Any positive number can be undercut by choosing a slightly smaller one, so eventually all reasoning leads to O. 9

=== Page 10 ===
Remark 2 In the first game: Some chose 33: they take the midpoint between O and 100 (50), then compute - x 50 33.33. This is called reasoning. Others chose 22: they anticipate that others will pick 33, so they answer - x 33 reasoning. They have a level 2 Some may reason even further (Level-3, Level-4 ) This is called level-k theory, and it is part of behavioral game theory, Study of how people actually play games, incorporating bounded rationality, limited 22. reasoning steps, social preferences, and learning, rather than assuming perfectly rational Nash equilibrium Play. 10

Remark 3 In Game 2: The payoff rule changed: winners shared -A. This gave everyone an incentive to raise their numbers, since a higher average means a larger prize to share. But: The Nash equilibrium remains the same everyone choosing O. We still observe the trend towards lower and lower answers across rounds. The tension: Collectively, it would be better if everyone played high the prize grows. Individually, each Player has a temptation to deviate downward, to be the closest and capture the prize. 16

Remark 4 If everyone agreed to play 1000, they would all share about 6667. But a single player could break their promise and deviate to 999 and win almost 6667 alone. If everyone expects 999, the best reply is 998... If everyone expects 998, the best reply is 997... and so on. By iterating this reasoning, the game still converges to the equilibrium: O.
